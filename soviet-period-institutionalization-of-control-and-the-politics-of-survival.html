Soviet Period: Institutionalization of Control and the Politics of Survival

Shtan Bohdan 29/04/2026

 

After the occupation of Ukraine by the USSR (1922–1991), Ukraine formally maintained the status of a union republic, but in practice, the union was a new form of the Moscow empire.

  • Holodomor (1932–1933): A man-made famine used as genocide against the Ukrainian people. Directives emphasized: "ensure the fulfillment of the grain procurement plan at any cost." The use of "Blacklists" (blocking villages from food supply) intensified the tragedy. Estimates of victims range from 2.6 million to 11 million. The National Academy of Sciences of Ukraine cites 4.5 million victims.

  • Repressions and the "Executed Renaissance": In the 1930s, the Soviet regime liquidated a significant part of the Ukrainian cultural elite. Figures like Mykola Khvylovy, Les Kurbas, and Mykola Kulish were executed or forced into silence. The total number of repressed cultural figures exceeds 30,000.

  • Russification: In the 1930s, the "Ukrainization" policy was replaced by harsh Russification—a planned ethnocide. Methods included the Spelling Reform of 1933 (to make Ukrainian more like Russian), the 1958 Education Reform, and financial incentives for Russian language teachers (1984).
