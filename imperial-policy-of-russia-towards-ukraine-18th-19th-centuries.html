Imperial Policy of Russia Towards Ukraine (18th–19th Centuries)

Shtan Bohdan 29/04/2026

 

After the liquidation of the Hetmanate's autonomy in the second half of the 18th century, the Russian Empire moved toward the systemic integration of Ukrainian lands. This included administrative centralization and the gradual transformation of the cultural and linguistic space.

The abolition of the Hetmanate (1764) and the liquidation of the Zaporozhian Sich (1775) meant the dismantling of political institutions that maintained the distinctiveness of Ukrainian society. Language policy played a special role as a tool for forming a unified imperial identity.

  • Valuev Circular (1863): An internal document of the Russian government:
    "...Никакого малоросийского языка не было, нет, и быть не может..." (original)
    "...there was not, is not, and cannot be any special Little Russian (Ukrainian) language..."     This phrase is key; it does not just limit the language but denies the very fact of its existence.

  • Ems Ukaz (1876): Signed by Emperor Alexander II, it prohibited: the printing of books in Ukrainian, importing Ukrainian publications from abroad, theatrical performances in Ukrainian, and the use of the language in education. It also denied the existence of Ukrainian by calling it a "Little Russian dialect."
