The Hetmanate Period: The Beginning of Absorption and First Prohibitions (18th Century)

Shtan Bohdan 29/04/2026

The process of destroying Ukrainian agency began long before the term "Russification" appeared, through direct war crimes and church censorship.

  • The Baturyn Tragedy (1708): After Hetman Ivan Mazepa (head of the Ukrainian state in the late 17th and early 18th centuries) attempted to withdraw from Moscow's protectorate, Russian troops under Menshikov completely destroyed the capital of the Hetmanate. All inhabitants were killed, including infants and the elderly (approx. 11,000–15,000 people). This was the first large-scale punitive action intended to intimidate Ukrainians.

  • Anathema on Mazepa (1708): By order of Peter I, the Russian Orthodox Church placed a curse on the Hetman, which was read in all churches for over 200 years. This was an example of ideological warfare and the use of religion to discredit the idea of independence. Furthermore, all portraits of Mazepa within the Russian Empire were destroyed—a blow to Ukrainian culture today, as Mazepa led the state for over 20 years.

  • Prohibition of Printing (1720): A decree by Peter I forbade Ukrainian printing houses from publishing any books except religious ones, and their text had to be brought into full compliance with Russian standards, "So that there would be no difference or dialect."

Social and Economic Slavery

  • Enslavement (1783): A decree by Catherine II officially introduced serfdom in Left-bank and Sloboda Ukraine. Free Cossacks and peasants effectively became the property of imperial masters. This was a way to break the freedom-loving spirit of the people, turning them into a resource without rights.

  • Rewriting History: Catherine II created the "Commission for the Compilation of Notes on Ancient History, Primarily of Russia." It was then that the mass falsification of chronicles began to appropriate the heritage of Kyivan Rus' and prove that Ukrainians are merely a "spoiled part" of the Russian people.
